# Scope

This section defines **what** the Master Lifecycle procedure applies to, **where** its boundaries are, and **how** it relates to the numbered phase documents and **USSM v1.0**.

For why the procedure exists and who uses it, see **`02. Purpose.md`**. For documentation tiers, USSM mapping, and traditional SDLC alignment, see **`06. Lifecycle Overview.md`**.

---

## 3.1 Applicability

This procedure applies to **CYBERCUBE software work** managed as a project or product evolution, from initial idea capture through **Post-Release Review**.

The procedure includes selection, requirements, planning, design, implementation, validation, release, deployment, maintenance, and lessons learned.

Scaling follows **`02. Purpose.md`** §**1.4**. All projects follow the same lifecycle phases, but the depth of documentation shall match project risk, criticality, complexity, and regulatory need.

---

## 3.2 In Scope

The procedure governs:

| Area | Coverage |
|---|---|
| Lifecycle | Fourteen ordered phases with **decision gates**. Projects shall not advance without sufficient understanding, required outputs, and approvals. See §**3.5**. |
| Documentation and evidence | Required artifacts, production timing, and traceability chains anchored in **USSM v1.0** and detailed in **`22. Required Documents.md`**. |
| Overlays and subprocedures | Domain-specific rules, such as module design, component design, blueprints, and implementation addenda. These overlays reference this lifecycle and **do not replace** it. |

---

## 3.3 Out of Scope and Boundaries

The following are **not** defined by this Master Lifecycle procedure. Other CYBERCUBE standards, policies, or runbooks apply:

* **Binding technical requirements** for security, privacy, cryptography, authentication, authorization, observability, infrastructure, and similar technical domains. This procedure **orchestrates** when and how those controls appear in the lifecycle; the relevant technical standards define the **what**.
* **Organization-wide policies**, including HR, finance, procurement, legal administration, and physical security, except where software delivery must **evidence** compliance through records, approvals, or required artifacts.
* **Work explicitly governed** by a different **approved** procedure, tailoring decision, or waiver recorded under governance, with traceability to that decision.

Pure **hardware-only** products without a software lifecycle are outside this procedure unless software delivery becomes part of the same initiative.

## 3.5 Lifecycle Control Principle

A project **shall not** move to the next phase until the current phase has produced **sufficient evidence** to justify continuation.

At each transition, the team shall answer:

1. Do we understand enough to continue?
2. Have we produced the **required outputs** for this phase?
3. Have we passed the required **review or decision gate**?
4. Are risks, open questions, and unresolved issues documented?

Required outputs are defined by the applicable phase document and **`22. Required Documents.md`**.

If the answer is no, the project shall pause, return to an earlier phase, adjust scope, or stop. The lifecycle is **not** a rubber stamp.
