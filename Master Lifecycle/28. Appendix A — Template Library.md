# Appendix A — Template Library

## Purpose and Template Index

The **Templates Index** for CRS, SRS, DDS, test plans, deployment plans, maintenance logs, traceability matrices, change requests, compliance checklists, glossary pages, and metadata shells is specified in **USSM Section 10.6 — Annex F** (`USSM — Unified Software Standards Manual v1.0.md`).

Centralize controlled templates in a single repository, version them with the rest of the procedure, and update them when USSM or governing standards change.

This appendix is the Master Lifecycle's reusable evidence library. It contains templates for discovery through post-release review: **A-0 through A-41**, plus **A-25.1**. Some entries are full templates; others are pointers to standalone controlled procedures where the canonical template already exists.

## Template Details

The following templates consolidate controlled lifecycle evidence. Use them when a project needs consistent structure, approval records, section numbering, traceability hooks, and gate evidence compatible with the Master Lifecycle and USSM.

### Template Metadata Convention

Every completed lifecycle artifact should include stable metadata near the top of the document unless the template provides a stricter header:

```text
Artifact ID:
Template ID:
Artifact Title:
Related Product ID (PRD-XXX), if any:
Related PCL Code, if any:
Lifecycle Phase:
Related Gate(s):
Owner:
Prepared By:
Version / Revision:
Date:
Status:
```

Apply `STD-ENG-001` as follows:

- Use lifecycle-local artifact IDs for ordinary lifecycle evidence unless a CYBERCUBE governance registry assigns a Namespace G ID.
- Use `PRD-XXX`, `MOD-XXX`, or future Namespace G IDs only when assigned by the authoritative registry.
- Use CC-PID for public entity identifiers where APIs, URLs, logs, support workflows, or UI expose entity references; do not expose raw database primary keys externally.
- Follow Namespace M conventions for module, component, directory, export, and file names in implementation-facing templates.
- If an existing artifact cannot use this metadata block, record an equivalent mapping in the traceability matrix.

### Template A-0 — Idea Capture Form

**Purpose:** Record a software idea before it becomes an approved project. The form captures the early idea, the problem it may solve, target users, expected value, current situation, high-level solution, risks, constraints, complexity, recommendation, and approval status. The form does **not** approve the project.

**Lifecycle:** Produced in **Phase 1 — Idea Capture** and reviewed at **G1 — Idea Accepted for Analysis** before moving to **Phase 2 — Problem Definition**.

**Primary owner:** Idea Sponsor (the person who proposes the idea).

**Supporting reviewers:**

| Role | Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| Project Owner | Reviews fit with business goals. |
| Technical Lead | Provides early technical feasibility comments. |
| Product / Business Reviewer | Evaluates potential customer or business value. |
| Standards / Governance Reviewer | Confirms the idea should continue into the formal lifecycle. |

#### Required sections

1. Idea Title
2. Idea Sponsor and Date Submitted
3. Short Idea Summary
4. Problem or Opportunity
5. Target Users or Beneficiaries
6. Current Situation
7. Expected Benefit
8. Possible Software Solution
9. Urgency or Timing
10. Known Constraints
11. Known Risks or Concerns
12. Similar Existing Solutions
13. Initial Complexity Estimate
14. Initial PRCS Context
15. Recommendation
16. Approval Status

#### Form body

##### 1. Idea Title — Required

Provide a short, clear name for the idea.

```text
Idea Title:
```

##### 2. Idea Sponsor and Date Submitted — Required

Identify who proposed the idea and when.

```text
Submitted By:
Role:
Contact:
Date Submitted:
```

##### 3. Short Idea Summary — Required

Describe the idea in 2–4 sentences.

```text
Summary:
```

##### 4. Problem or Opportunity — Required

Describe the problem, pain point, inefficiency, opportunity, or need that inspired the idea.

```text
Problem / Opportunity:
```

Helpful questions: What is happening? Why does it matter? Who is affected? What gets worse if nothing changes?

##### 5. Target Users or Beneficiaries — Required

Identify who would use or benefit from the software.

```text
Primary Users:
Secondary Users:
Business Beneficiaries:
```

Examples: small business owners, internal employees, customers, administrators, managers, nonprofit staff, field workers, students, developers.

##### 6. Current Situation — Required

Explain how the problem is handled today.

```text
Current Process / Current Solution:
```

Helpful questions: Is the work manual? Spreadsheet-based? Email-based? Handled by another system? What is frustrating or inefficient?

##### 7. Expected Benefit — Required

Describe the value this idea could create.

```text
Expected Benefit:
```

Possible benefits: saves time, reduces errors, improves customer experience, increases revenue, reduces cost, improves reporting, automates repetitive work, improves compliance, improves visibility, improves decision-making.

##### 8. Possible Software Solution — Required

Describe the possible software solution at a high level. Stay simple — do **not** design the full system yet.

```text
Possible Solution:
```

Example: *A small web portal where customers can submit service requests and track their status.*

##### 9. Urgency or Timing — Required

```text
Urgency:        (Low | Medium | High | Critical)
Timing Notes:
```

##### 10. Known Constraints — Required if known

List any limitations that may affect the project.

```text
Known Constraints:
```

Examples: limited budget, limited development time, must integrate with existing system, must support mobile users, must follow security requirements, must be ready before a specific date, requires customer approval, requires third-party API.

##### 11. Known Risks or Concerns — Required if known

```text
Known Risks / Concerns:
```

Examples: unclear user need, unknown technical complexity, possible privacy concerns, integration may be difficult, high maintenance burden, no confirmed customer demand.

##### 12. Similar Existing Solutions — Optional but recommended

List any existing tools, competitors, internal systems, or manual processes that already solve part of the problem.

```text
Similar Existing Solutions:
```

Helpful questions: Is there already software for this? Why is the existing option insufficient? Could we buy instead of build? Could we improve an existing tool?

##### 13. Initial Complexity Estimate — Required

Provide an early rough estimate. See `23. Project Complexity Levels.md` for the canonical rubric.

```text
Initial Complexity:    (Small | Medium | Large | Unknown)
```

##### 14. Initial PRCS Context — Required if known

Record early product-registry context without requiring final classification at G1.

```text
Existing Product ID (PRD-XXX), if any:
Candidate New Product? Yes / No / Unknown:
Candidate Product / Service Name:
Tentative PCL Code (PCL-L.D.E.C), if known:
Likely Domain Tags:
Function Descriptor Notes:
Work Type Tag(s), if pre-product or cross-cutting:
PRCS Notes / Unknowns:
```

##### 15. Recommendation — Required

State what should happen next.

```text
Recommendation:    (Reject | Defer | Research More | Move to Problem Definition)
```

##### 16. Approval Status — Required

Record the current status of the idea.

```text
Approval Status:
Reviewer:
Review Date:
Decision Notes:
```

| Status | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Draft | The idea is still being written. |
| Submitted | The idea has been formally submitted for review. |
| Under Review | A reviewer is evaluating whether it should continue. |
| Accepted for Problem Definition | The idea may move to Phase 2. |
| Research More | The idea needs additional input before G1 can be decided. |
| Deferred | The idea is valid but will not continue now. |
| Rejected | The idea will not continue. |

#### Completion criteria

The Idea Capture Form is complete when every required section above has a value, the recommendation is recorded, and an approval status has been assigned by a named reviewer with a review date. Vague, unscoped, or unowned ideas must be returned to the sponsor before the form is accepted.

### Template A-0.1 — Problem Definition Document

**Purpose:** Define the real problem behind a software idea before the project is evaluated, selected, planned, or designed. The document ensures work rests on a **real need**, not only an interesting feature, assumption, trend, or technology idea.

**Lifecycle:** Produced in **Phase 2 — Problem Definition** and reviewed at **G2 — Problem Validated** before moving to **Phase 3 — Project Evaluation and Selection**. Approval requires at least one independent validation source.

**Primary owner:** Project Owner or Idea Sponsor.

**Supporting owners:**

| Role | Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| Idea Sponsor | Explains the original idea and motivation. |
| Project Owner | Owns the problem definition and review process. |
| Business reviewer | Confirms business relevance and expected value. |
| User / customer representative | Confirms whether the problem is real. |
| Technical lead | Identifies early technical concerns if needed. |
| Governance reviewer | Confirms the document is complete enough to continue. |

For a solo project, the same person may perform several roles.

#### Required sections

1. Document Metadata
2. Problem Title
3. Problem Summary
4. Background and Context
5. Affected Users or Stakeholders
6. Current Process or Current Solution
7. Pain Points and Limitations
8. Problem Frequency
9. Problem Severity
10. Evidence or Observations
11. Business Impact
12. User Impact
13. Operational Impact
14. Why the Current Solution Is Insufficient
15. Desired Outcome
16. Initial Success Indicators
17. Assumptions
18. Constraints
19. Risks of Not Solving the Problem
20. Out of Scope
21. Recommendation
22. Approval Status

#### Form body

##### 1. Document Metadata

```text
Document Title:
Project / Idea Name:
Document Version:
Status:
Owner:
Prepared By:
Date Created:
Last Updated:
Related Idea Capture Form:
```

Suggested statuses: Draft · Submitted · Under Review · Validated — Proceed to Selection · Return to Phase 1 · Deferred · Rejected

##### 2. Problem Title

Provide a short, clear title for the problem.

```text
Problem Title:
```

Example: *Small contractors lose billable equipment usage because telematics data is not connected to invoicing.*

##### 3. Problem Summary

Describe the problem in one short paragraph (who, what is wrong, why it matters, consequence).

```text
Problem Summary:
```

Example: *Small equipment rental and wet-hire companies often track machine usage manually or across disconnected systems. Because operating hours, idle time, attachment usage, and fuel data are not automatically connected to invoices, billable usage may be missed, disputed, or delayed, creating revenue loss, administrative burden, and weaker billing accuracy.*

##### 4. Background and Context

Explain the situation that created the problem.

```text
Background / Context:
```

Helpful questions: Where does it happen? When? Who is involved? What workflow creates the issue? What tools or manual steps are used?

##### 5. Affected Users or Stakeholders

```text
Primary Users Affected:
Secondary Users Affected:
Business Stakeholders:
External Stakeholders:
```

Examples: customers, employees, managers, administrators, field workers, accounting staff, support teams, compliance reviewers.

##### 6. Current Process or Current Solution

```text
Current Process / Current Solution:
```

Helpful questions: Manual? Spreadsheets? Email? Phone? Existing software? Depends on one person's memory? Data copied between systems?

##### 7. Pain Points and Limitations

```text
Pain Points:
```

Examples: manual data entry; duplicated work; no real-time status; errors discovered late; slow reports; customers must call for updates; records hard to find; spreadsheet dependence; weak audit trail; lost time, money, or visibility.

##### 8. Problem Frequency

```text
Problem Frequency:
```

Suggested values: Rare · Occasional · Frequent · Daily · Continuous · Unknown

```text
Frequency Explanation:
```

Example: *Weekly during invoice preparation; daily when field usage data is manually reviewed.*

##### 9. Problem Severity

```text
Problem Severity:
```

Suggested values: Low · Medium · High · Critical · Unknown

| Severity | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Low | Annoying but little operational effect. |
| Medium | Delays, confusion, moderate inefficiency. |
| High | Revenue loss, customer frustration, compliance risk, or major burden. |
| Critical | Blocks operations, serious legal/security risk, or major financial damage. |

```text
Severity Explanation:
```

##### 10. Evidence or Observations

```text
Evidence / Observations:
Validation Source(s):
```

Evidence may include: user complaints; interviews; support tickets; screenshots; estimates; time tracking; errors; analytics; incidents; research; observation.

If no independent validation source exists yet:

```text
Evidence Status: Not yet validated
Additional Research Needed:
```

##### 11. Business Impact

```text
Business Impact:
```

Possible impacts: revenue loss; labor cost; delays; poor CX; lost productivity; missed opportunities; compliance exposure; bad reporting; slow decisions; higher support load.

##### 12. User Impact

```text
User Impact:
```

Does it waste time? Cause frustration? Force rework? Create confusion? Drive support dependence? Block tasks?

##### 13. Operational Impact

```text
Operational Impact:
```

Examples: more manual work; delayed approvals; poor visibility; fragmented records; inconsistent processes; harder maintenance.

##### 14. Why the Current Solution Is Insufficient

```text
Why Current Solution Is Insufficient:
```

What fails today? Why not scale? Unreliable? Too slow? Error-prone? Too costly? Frustrating?

##### 15. Desired Outcome

Describe the **result**, not the detailed software solution.

```text
Desired Outcome:
```

Good: *The business calculates accurate billable equipment usage with less manual work and fewer invoice disputes.*
Weak: *Build a dashboard with five tabs and a blue button.*

##### 16. Initial Success Indicators

```text
Initial Success Indicators:
```

Examples: reduced manual entry; shorter invoice prep time; fewer disputes; fewer support requests; automated reports; adoption targets met.

##### 17. Assumptions

```text
Assumptions:
```

Examples: users will adopt a portal; required data is accessible; integration API exists; manual process causes measurable delay.

##### 18. Constraints

```text
Constraints:
```

Examples: budget; timeline; team size; required integrations; mobile need; security rules; roles; data sensitivity.

##### 19. Risks of Not Solving the Problem

```text
Risks of Not Solving:
```

Examples: continued revenue loss; persistent manual work; frustration; errors; support burden; missed market opportunity; compliance exposure; lost competitiveness.

##### 20. Out of Scope

```text
Out of Scope:
```

Examples: final architecture; technology stack; detailed UI; schema; implementation plan; pricing; timeline; full feature list, so this phase does not become design.

##### 21. Recommendation

```text
Recommendation:
```

Suggested values: Validated — Proceed to Selection · Return to Phase 1 · Defer · Reject

```text
Recommendation Rationale:
```

##### 22. Approval Status

```text
Approval Status:
Reviewer:
Review Date:
Decision Notes:
```

| Status | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Draft | Still being written. |
| Submitted | Submitted for review. |
| Under Review | Reviewer evaluating. |
| Validated — Proceed to Selection | Clear enough and independently validated to proceed to Phase 3. |
| Return to Phase 1 | The idea/problem framing is wrong or insufficient and should return to intake. |
| Deferred | Valid but not continuing now. |
| Rejected | Will not continue. |

#### Completion criteria

The Problem Definition Document is complete when:

1. The problem has a clear title and plain-language summary.
2. Background and context are documented.
3. Affected users and stakeholders are identified.
4. The current process or solution is described.
5. Pain points, frequency, and severity are recorded.
6. At least one independent validation source is documented for G2 approval, or the document is explicitly not ready for G2 approval.
7. Business, user, and operational impacts are explained.
8. Insufficiency of the current approach is explained; desired outcome is stated.
9. Initial success indicators are defined; assumptions and constraints listed.
10. Risks of not solving and out-of-scope items are documented.
11. A recommendation is recorded and approval status assigned by a named reviewer with date.

### Template A-2 — Software Requirement Specification (SRS)

**Purpose:** Translate CRS into **testable**, engineering-ready requirements (objectives, detailed requirements, assumptions, risks).

**Typical outline**

1. **Introduction** — Purpose; intended audience; intended use; product scope; definitions and acronyms.
2. **Overall description** — User needs; assumptions and dependencies.
3. **System features and requirements** — Functional requirements; external interface requirements; system features; non-functional requirements.

**Collaborative SRS shaping:** Workshop and refine **project objectives**, detailed **requirements**, **anticipated challenges**, **opportunities**, and **risks** so the SRS doubles as the aligned engineering vision—not only a flat requirement list.

**Alternate section numbering (IEEE-style introduction)**

Some teams prefer explicit numbering compatible with classic SRS templates:

1. Introduction — 1.1 Purpose · 1.2 Intended audience · 1.3 Intended use · 1.4 Product scope · 1.5 Definitions and acronyms  
2. Overall description — 2.1 User needs · 2.2 Assumptions and dependencies  
3. System features and requirements — 3.1 Functional · 3.2 External interface · 3.3 System features · 3.4 Non-functional requirements  

Either numbering style is acceptable if applied consistently and trace IDs remain stable.

#### Introduction — what to establish

- **Intended audience and use:** Who reads the SRS (engineering, QA, PM, stakeholders) and how they use it.
- **Product scope:** Benefits, objectives, goals tied to business outcomes.
- **Definitions:** Terms and acronyms; link to a glossary when terminology is heavy.

#### Overall description — product narrative

- Why the product is needed; who it is for; new vs. add-on vs. integration with other products.
- **User needs:** Primary vs. secondary users; organizational roles; purchaser vs. end user; domain-specific users (e.g. patient in regulated domains).
- **Assumptions and dependencies:** Technical assumptions (platform, stack); dependencies on other systems or reused components.

#### Detailed requirements — categories

- **Functional requirements:** Capabilities the product must provide; domain-specific subsets where applicable; interaction with other tools.
- **External interface requirements:** How the product connects to users, hardware, software, communications (especially embedded systems).
- **System features:** Required capabilities for the system to function as specified.
- **Non-functional requirements:** Performance, safety, security, usability, scalability; sector-specific regulations may mandate explicit safety/traceability.

Use IEEE or organizational guidance for SRS authoring where membership or standards apply.

### Template A-4 — Business Field Report

**Purpose:** Analyze a **business field** (market, industry, or opportunity space) so the organization can make **informed portfolio and funding decisions**. Standard sections support **Phase 3 — Project Evaluation and Selection** and inform **Phase 4 — Feasibility and Business Case**.

**Audience:** Tailor depth and tone to executives, sponsors, product leadership, or portfolio reviewers—state the intended audience in the introduction.

**Quality bar:** Credible sources, clear citations, concise narrative, visuals where they clarify quant trends, and **specific recommendations** tied to evidence.

#### Core sections (outline)

| Section | Content |
| --- | --- |
| **Executive summary** | Findings, conclusions, and recommendations (standalone readable). |
| **Introduction** | Field definition, scope, relevance to the organization. |
| **Market analysis** | Size, growth, segmentation, trends; demographics and customer profiles; behavior (needs, buying habits, brand perception); primary vs secondary research methods; benefits of structured market research. |
| **Competitive analysis** | Major players, share, strategies; strengths/weaknesses; differentiation / USP; **financial view** (statements, ratios, benchmarking, trends)—see Financial analysis subset below. |
| **Future outlook** | Forecast trends; recommendations for players in the space. |
| **SWOT** | Strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats for the **industry or opportunity context**; prioritize factors; derive strategies, options, resource focus, and risk responses. |
| **Trends and challenges** | Notable drivers (technology, digital transformation, ESG, globalization, remote work, data-driven decisions, CX) and pressures (economy, supply chain, cyber, talent, regulation, climate, geopolitical risk). |
| **Potential growth areas** | Innovation, expansion, technology, operational excellence, CX, sustainability/ESG; techniques (research, SWOT, scenarios, workshops, customer feedback). |
| **Recommendations** | Aligned to goals, feasible, measurable, risk-aware, prioritized—product/service, markets, operations, CX, digital, sustainability as relevant. |
| **Conclusion** | Restate main conclusions and next decisions. |

#### Financial analysis subset (within competitive or industry analysis)

Use when public or internal financial data exists for relevant companies:

1. Gather statements (income, balance sheet, cash flow).
2. Compute ratios: profitability (e.g. gross/net margin, ROE, ROA), liquidity (current, quick), solvency (debt-to-equity, interest coverage), efficiency (inventory and receivables turnover, as applicable).
3. Benchmark against peers and industry norms.
4. Trend over periods; interpret with judgment.

#### Specialized variants (reuse the same evidence standards)

**Market entry analysis** — Assess entering a new geography or segment: market assessment; regulatory/IP; economics (macro, FX, infrastructure); culture and behavior; entry modes (export, licensing, franchise, JV, FDI, etc.); risk (political, economic, currency, operational).

**Emerging technology report** — Identify and assess technologies: maturity, feasibility and viability, market potential, competitive landscape, regulation, ethics, future outlook and organizational implications.

**Customer segmentation study** — Demographic, psychographic, behavioral, needs-based segments; steps: collect data → analyze → identify segments → profile → prioritize.

**Competitive landscape analysis** — Direct vs indirect competitors; share and strategies; SWOT per competitor where useful; customer and financial views; outlook and implications.

### Template A-5 — Entrepreneurial forecasting guide

**Purpose:** Document **defensible financial and market projections** for **Phase 4 — Feasibility and Business Case** (economic feasibility, cash and runway) and establish habits for **actual-vs-forecast** review in **Phase 13 — Maintenance and Improvement**.

**Scope:** Revenue, expenses, cash timing, market trend assumptions, growth scenarios—not a substitute for statutory accounting or audited statements.

#### What to estimate

| Area | Typical questions |
| --- | --- |
| Revenue | Income scale and timing (subscriptions, usage, services). |
| Expenses | People, infrastructure, sales/marketing, tooling, overhead. |
| Market trends | Demand, competition, regulation. |
| Growth | Customers, usage, expansion paths. |

#### Why forecasting matters

- Capital and runway planning  
- Risk surfacing and mitigation  
- Sponsor or investor alignment  
- Hiring, pricing, and roadmap decisions  
- Benchmarks for **later** performance evaluation (Phase 13)  

#### Types

| Type | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Sales | Pipeline and historical conversion to future periods. |
| Expense | Fixed and variable costs by category. |
| Cash flow | When cash moves—not only accrual profit. |
| Market | External shifts affecting demand or compliance costs. |

#### Methods

| Approach | When |
| --- | --- |
| **Qualitative** | Sparse history—expert input, customer research, **scenario planning** (what-if cases). |
| **Quantitative** | Reliable history—trends, smoothing (e.g. moving averages), regression between drivers (e.g. marketing spend and qualified leads). |

#### Steps

1. **Define goals** — Metrics and horizon (monthly / quarterly / annual).  
2. **Gather data** — Internal actuals and external benchmarks.  
3. **Choose methods** — Match data maturity and uncertainty.  
4. **State assumptions** — Seasonality, pricing, competition, regulatory steps; prefer documented ranges.  
5. **Build scenarios** — At minimum **best**, **likely**, and **worst** cases tied to named drivers.  
6. **Monitor and revise** — Compare actuals on a cadence; update models when reality diverges (handoff to Phase 13).  

#### Tooling (examples)

Spreadsheets; accounting systems for actuals; forecasting/planning apps; CRM or billing analytics for revenue drivers—pick tools your finance and ops teams can sustain.

#### Common mistakes

Overstated revenue; ignored taxes, inflation, or step costs; frozen forecasts despite market change; weak competitor/regulatory awareness; intuition without data checks.

#### Practices

Start with tractable horizons; segment by product, region, or customer where material; involve cross-functional inputs; use industry benchmarks where valid; reserve contingency scenarios for shocks.
