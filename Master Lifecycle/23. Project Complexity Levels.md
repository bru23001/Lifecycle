# Project Complexity Levels

This document provides the lightweight complexity rubric used by early lifecycle phases. It helps teams scale documentation, review depth, and gate evidence without skipping required lifecycle phases.

For lifecycle applicability and scaling rules, see **`03. Scope.md`** and **`02. Purpose.md`** §**1.4**. Phase documents may request a rough complexity estimate before enough information exists for a full assessment; in that case, use **Unknown** and record what must be learned next.

---

## 1. Quick Complexity Rubric

| Level | Typical indicators | Lifecycle treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Small** | Simple feature, small website, small automation, limited users, no sensitive data, low integration burden. | Use the standard lifecycle with lightweight artifacts and minimal review depth. |
| **Medium** | Multi-screen app, database-backed system, moderate users, basic integrations, moderate operational or support needs. | Use standard artifacts with clear requirements, design, testing, release evidence, and named owners. |
| **Large** | SaaS platform, regulated or security-sensitive system, multiple roles, complex integrations, high availability needs, significant data handling, or cross-functional delivery. | Use fuller documentation, stronger gate evidence, security/privacy/architecture review, and explicit operational planning. |
| **Unknown** | Insufficient information to classify complexity, unclear users, unclear data, unknown integrations, or uncertain regulatory/operational exposure. | Continue discovery/research before treating the idea as ready for downstream planning. |

---

## 2. Examples and Required Documents by Level

The legacy planning procedure names the three practical documentation levels as **Level 1 — Small Project**, **Level 2 — Standard Project**, and **Level 3 — Critical / Governed Project**. In this document, those map to **Small**, **Medium**, and **Large** respectively.

### 2.1 Level 1 — Small Project

**Typical examples:**

- Small website.
- Internal tool.
- Simple automation.
- One-person MVP.

**Minimum required documents:**

```text
Problem Definition
Scope Document
Feature List
Basic Design
Test Checklist
Deployment Checklist
Post-Release Review
```

Use lightweight versions of these artifacts where risk is low, scope is narrow, users are limited, and no sensitive data, production-critical workflow, regulated process, multi-tenant data, or security-sensitive surface is involved.

### 2.2 Level 2 — Standard Project

**Typical examples:**

- Business web app.
- CRM portal.
- SaaS MVP.
- Mobile app.
- Database-backed internal or customer-facing workflow.

**Minimum required documents:**

```text
Problem Definition
Project Selection Scorecard
Requirements Specification
Architecture Document
UI/UX Design
Data Model
Development Plan
Test Strategy
Release Checklist
Maintenance Plan
```

Use standard lifecycle artifacts with named owners, traceability seeds, design review, testing evidence, release readiness, and maintenance ownership.

### 2.3 Level 3 — Critical / Governed Project

**Typical examples:**

- Compliance system.
- Healthcare app.
- Financial system.
- Security-sensitive platform.
- Multi-tenant SaaS.
- Regulated, high-availability, or customer-data platform.

**Minimum required documents:**

```text
Full lifecycle documentation
Security review
Risk register
Traceability matrix
Compliance matrix
Architecture decision records
Formal release approval
Operational monitoring plan
Incident response plan
```

Use full lifecycle evidence, stronger review depth, explicit risk ownership, security/privacy/compliance validation, operational planning, and formal release approval. Required CYBERCUBE standards evidence is governed by `25. Quality and Compliance Checks.md` §5.

## 3. Early Estimate Fields

When a phase asks for a complexity estimate, record:

- **Initial complexity:** Small / Medium / Large / Unknown.
- **Rationale:** 1–3 sentences explaining the estimate.
- **Main drivers:** users, data sensitivity, integrations, availability, compliance, team size, or delivery uncertainty.
- **Unknowns:** information needed to confirm or revise the estimate.

The canonical early estimate field appears in **Template A-0 — Idea Capture Form** in **`28. Appendix A — Template Library.md`**. Later phases may revise the estimate as requirements, architecture, data, integration, security, and operational facts become clearer.

---

## 4. Lifecycle Selection Checkpoints

Complexity is selected progressively. Early phases may use **Unknown** or a provisional level; by the end of Phase 4, the project should have a confirmed level or an explicit owner for resolving remaining uncertainty.

| Lifecycle point | Complexity action | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phase 1 — Idea Capture | Select an initial complexity estimate: Small / Medium / Large / Unknown. | Idea Capture Form, initial rationale, known risks. |
| Phase 2 — Problem Definition | Re-check the estimate against validated users, severity, constraints, and evidence. | Problem Definition Document, evidence/observations, updated rationale if changed. |
| Phase 3 — Project Evaluation and Selection | Use complexity as an evaluation input for scoring, cost/benefit, risk, delivery effort, and review depth. | Project Selection Scorecard or equivalent evaluation record. |
| Phase 4 — Feasibility and Business Case | Confirm the complexity level before G3, or record why it remains Unknown and what must be resolved. | Feasibility Assessment, Business Case, CYBERCUBE standards applicability evidence. |
| Phase 5 — Requirements Definition | Apply the confirmed complexity level to requirements/NFR depth, traceability rigor, review depth, and required documents. | Requirements Specification Package, NFR register, traceability seed. |

After Phase 5, complexity may still be revised if design, implementation, testing, release, or operational evidence reveals higher risk. Any downgrade requires rationale; any upgrade must update affected artifacts and gate evidence.

## 5. Scaling Matrix

Complexity changes the depth of evidence, not the lifecycle path.

| Area | Small | Medium | Large |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Requirements | Lightweight but testable CRS/SRS sections; explicit assumptions. | Standard Requirements Specification Package with traceability seed. | Full requirements package with stronger traceability, stakeholder review, and NFR coverage. |
| Architecture | Simple design notes or ARD-001-lite where architecture is straightforward. | ARD-001 plus key ADRs and data/API/UI artifacts where applicable. | Full architecture review, ADR coverage, data/API/UI/module artifacts, and explicit operational design. |
| Security / privacy | Basic secure-coding and data checks; no sensitive data by default. | Security/privacy review where data, auth, integrations, or operational risk exist. | Security, privacy, compliance, tenant/data isolation, and release security evidence expected unless formally waived. |
| Testing | Focused unit/integration/acceptance evidence for changed scope. | Standard Test Strategy, acceptance scenarios, QA results, and traceability. | Full test strategy, broader regression/security/performance/accessibility coverage, and stronger release evidence. |
| Release / deployment | Lightweight release/deployment records if low impact. | Standard release readiness, rollback, deployment, and configuration evidence. | Full release, rollback, security review, deployment, migration, monitoring, and support handoff evidence. |
| Maintenance | Known issues and owner recorded. | Maintenance plan/backlog and metrics review for supported product work. | Full maintenance plan, incident/change records, metrics/actuals review, and post-release improvement loop. |

## 6. Criticality Overrides Complexity

Project complexity is not the same as product criticality. A small project can still require high rigor if it touches sensitive data, regulated workflows, authentication/authorization, production infrastructure, customer-facing availability, payments, tenancy, security controls, or legal/compliance commitments.

Use PRCS / product classification guidance when available:

- **Low criticality:** team-level review may be sufficient if no sensitive data, auth, compliance, or production risk exists.
- **Medium+ criticality:** architecture review and named ownership are expected.
- **High+ criticality:** security review, SLA/SLO definition, stronger testing, and release evidence are expected.
- **Regulated+ criticality:** compliance verification, audit trail, privacy/data handling review, and documented approvals are expected.
- **Safety-critical or equivalent risk:** CTO / executive sign-off and external or independent review may be required.

If complexity and criticality disagree, use the higher-rigor treatment. Do not use a **Small** complexity estimate to downgrade required security, privacy, data, authorization, release, or operational controls.

---

## 7. Unknown Resolution Rule

Use **Unknown** when the team lacks enough information to classify the work. Unknown is not a shortcut to lightweight treatment.

Before moving downstream, record:

- what is unknown,
- who owns the discovery action,
- what evidence will resolve it,
- when it must be resolved,
- which gate or phase will re-check the estimate.

Unresolved security, privacy, data classification, tenancy, compliance, integration, availability, migration, or production-impact unknowns should be treated at **Medium** or **Large** review depth until resolved.

---

## 8. Scaling Principle

All projects still follow the Master Lifecycle. Complexity changes the **depth** of evidence, review, and documentation; it does not remove required gates or allow phase skipping unless a documented tailoring decision or waiver applies.

Tailoring decisions and waivers must be explicit, time-bound where applicable, and traceable to the relevant gate evidence.
